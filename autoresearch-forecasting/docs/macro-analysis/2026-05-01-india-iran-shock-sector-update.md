Sector dispersion is sharpening — **oil producers, defence, capex, banks** widen the gap vs **IT, aviation, paints, and INR-sensitive importers**.

### Chain narrative

The chain runs cleanly: persistent ~$40 Brent overhang via India's 85% crude-import dependency forces a +1.6% of GDP CAD widening via simple arithmetic `[CAD identity]`. That widening, combined with Fed-driven USD strength `[Fed-EM transmission]`, has compressed INR ~8% over 2 months — *more* than the pure oil channel justifies, confirming that the Fed-EM amplifier (channel 4) is doing additional work. The CPI pass-through (channel 2) hasn't yet shown up in the data but is *baked in* arithmetically — the test is the 12-May April CPI print. If headline breaches 4.5%, the RBI cut cycle is not just paused, it's dead, and a tactical hike comes onto the table by June MPC. The fiscal channel (6) is mechanical and visible in the budget arithmetic now. The capital-flow channel (4) is the most procyclical: FII selling depresses INR, which makes INR-denominated assets less attractive, which triggers more FII selling. This loop doesn't break until *either* oil falls or the Fed pivots dovish — neither imminent.

**Most important update vs 17-Apr report:** Channel 4 (Fed-EM amplification) has materially intensified in the last 2 weeks. The 29-Apr Fed hold + four hawkish dissents removed the easing optionality the market was pricing. INR move from 93 → 95.25 is the visible footprint.

**Top-3 most-similar dimensions:** geopolitical_flag (war active), oil_zscore (supply shock), real_10y_yield (US restrictive in real terms).

**Differential diagnosis (most-different dimensions, mandatory):**

1. **`headline_inflation`: 3.4% today vs 9.8% peak in 2022.** This is the single biggest distinguishing feature. India-CPI has not yet absorbed the oil pass-through; even if it does, India's CPI basket (food-heavy, with healthy 2026 monsoon) limits the upside vs Europe's energy-heavy basket. **Implication:** India's RBI is not under the same cornered policy dilemma the ECB faced in 2022. There is room to wait.

2. **`growth`: 6.9% (India domestic) vs 1.0% (global, 2022).** India's GDP composition is dominated by domestic demand (consumption + capex), not energy-intensive exports like Germany/Italy in 2022. **Implication:** the same supply shock translates into a growth scare in Europe-2022 but a margin-compression event for *importers* in India-2026, with domestic demand cushioning aggregate GDP.

3. **`monetary_stance`: +0.5 today vs +1.2 in 2022.** Today's tightening is *unilateral* (Fed-only); in 2022 it was synchronised globally (Fed + ECB + BoE + RBI all hiking together). **Implication:** the global liquidity drain is half what it was in 2022 — partly explaining why HY OAS is +0.5z today vs +1.4z then, and why India's banking system isn't showing stress.

### Resolution narrative (2022)

In 2022, the Europe gas crisis ended via three things, in order: (a) mild winter Oct-22 to Mar-23 collapsing demand; (b) record LNG cargoes from US replacing Russian gas; (c) demand destruction in energy-intensive industry (chemicals, glass, fertiliser shut down 15-20% of capacity). The *equivalent* paths for the current Iran shock would be: (a) regime change / nuclear deal reopening Hormuz; (b) substitution to non-Gulf crude (US shale + Brazilian + Guyana production ramp); (c) demand destruction (already starting in EM importers like Sri Lanka, Pakistan).

| Dimension | Today | 1991 (Jul-91 peak) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| oil shock from regional war | YES | YES | both Gulf-region wars |
| oil import dependency >70% | YES | YES | structural |

**Differential:** every single financial-system indicator is opposite. The 1991 vibe is "an oil shock hit a fragile balance sheet with no shock absorbers." Today's is "an oil shock is hitting a strong balance sheet with multiple shock absorbers" `[heuristic — sovereign resilience]`. The shock pattern rhymes; the *outcome* won't.

### Calibration takeaway

The 2022 analogue is more applicable than 1991 by a wide margin (similarity 0.51 vs 0.74; lower is closer). But the differential — particularly that India's headline inflation has *not* yet broken out and the Fed-tightening side is *unilateral* not synchronised — implies the *policy response* should be milder than 2022 and the *equity drawdown* should be shallower. The dominant risk to this view is the 12-May April CPI print: a >4.5% reading would compress the inflation differential and pull this regime closer to the 2022 archetype.

## 7. Frameworks Used

Every framework that appeared as a tag in this report, with a 2-3 line definition. Look these up in `~/.cursor/skills/cross-regime-research-analyst/frameworks.md` for full worked examples.

| Tag | What it states (2-3 lines) |
|---|---|
| `[CAD identity]` | A country's current account balance equals (saving − investment) and equivalently (trade balance + net income + transfers). It is an accounting identity, not a behavioural model — every cross-border flow analysis starts here. Used here for: India CAD widening from oil shock; INR depreciation channel; pharma/IT exporter benefits from INR weakness. |
| `[Fed-EM transmission]` | US monetary tightening propagates to EM via three reinforcing channels: USD strength → EM FX, dollar-debt servicing burden, and risk-asset repricing. Median elasticity: DXY +10% → EM equities −10-15% over 3 months. Used here for: Fed-driven amplification of oil-only INR move; FII-flow channel running 2-3× the pure-oil elasticity. |
| `[USD pass-through]` | The mechanism by which a depreciating local currency translates into higher imported-goods prices, then into headline CPI. Empirical: 5% INR depreciation adds ~35 bps to India CPI when layered on energy shocks. Used here for: paints, FMCG, aviation input-cost compression; CPI projection for May-Aug. |
| `[real-vs-nominal]` | Decisions and welfare depend on real (inflation-adjusted) variables, but most reported data is nominal. Used here to distinguish India's 3.4% CPI (low in *both* terms — real RBI rate is +1.85%) from US 10y yield 4.5% (real ~+1.6%, restrictive). |
| `[sectoral balances]` | In a closed accounting system: (private surplus) + (public surplus) − (current account) ≡ 0. A government deficit must be matched by private surplus or external surplus. Used here for: India's expansionary capex underwriting private-sector demand resilience even as the external sector (oil-driven CAD) drains. |
| `[DuPont]` | ROE = NPM × ATO × Leverage. Decomposing ROE shows *which lever* a firm depends on, and therefore which macro shock is fragile to. Used here for: banks (leverage stable, NPM expanding via lower provisions); IT (NPM resilient via offshoring but ATO weak under demand contraction). |
| `[heuristic — RBI reaction function]` | RBI typically pauses/reverses cuts when Brent stays >$100 for 3+ months. Not a formal framework; an empirical pattern from 2008, 2018, 2022 episodes. |
| `[heuristic — fiscal pass-through]` | Mechanical: rising crude lifts LPG / fertiliser subsidy bills by ~₹15-20k Cr per $10 Brent move. Not behavioural — it's a budget arithmetic. |
| `[heuristic — sovereign resilience]` | EM macro fragility scales inversely with FX reserve cover (months of imports), inversely with USD-debt-to-GDP, and inversely with banking-system asset quality. India today scores favorably on all three. |
| `[heuristic — producer leverage]` | Commodity producers' earnings are highly geared to commodity price moves: a 50% Brent move from $80 → $120 can lift ONGC EBITDA by 60-80% because most costs are fixed. |
| `[heuristic — refining margin expansion]` | When crude is high *and* product spreads widen (like during a war shock), complex refiners (Reliance, RIL Jamnagar) capture both barrels. |
| `[heuristic — pricing-power monopoly]` | Companies with low elasticity-of-demand products (ITC cigarettes) absorb input cost increases via price hikes without volume loss. The opposite of INDIGO/ASIANPAINT. |
| `[heuristic — capacity discipline]` | When an industry has consolidated to 3-4 players and has not added capacity for years (Indian cement post-2018 consolidation), demand recovery translates faster into pricing power than in fragmented industries. |
| `[heuristic — regulated returns]` | Power transmission/generation under cost-plus regulation (POWERGRID 15.5% ROE) is uncorrelated to the macro cycle by design — they earn the same real return whether oil is $60 or $120. |
| `[heuristic — stretched-consumer channel]` | When real disposable income is squeezed by inflation, unsecured-lending portfolios (BAJFINANCE) see early-bucket delinquencies rise first. |
| `[heuristic — ALM]` | Insurers' investment book (mostly long-dated bonds) gets a yield tailwind when rates rise, but liability discount rates also adjust. SBILIFE/HDFCLIFE positioned long-duration so net effect is positive in current regime. |
